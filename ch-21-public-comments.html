21  Public Comments

Federal rulemaking is where many of the country’s most consequential data-policy fights are decided, and almost nobody you know has written a comment on one. The op-ed in Chapter 17 reaches a general reader. The report in Chapter 18 reaches a funder. The testimony in Chapter 20 reaches a committee that invited you in advance. A public comment on a proposed rule reaches the federal agency drafting the rule, on the agency’s schedule, in a form the agency is legally required to read, without anyone inviting you first. The door is open. Most of the profession walks past it.

This chapter treats the public comment as a genre. You will read a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM), find the specific questions the agency is asking, draft a substantive comment that cites the NPRM’s section numbers, and (with instructor approval) submit it through regulations.gov. The running example is one of the mid-2020s’ large algorithmic-accountability dockets: the Federal Trade Commission’s Commercial Surveillance and Data Security ANPRM (FTC-2022-0053, comment period closed November 2022), or the National Institute of Standards and Technology’s AI Risk Management Framework comment cycles from the same period. Both produced thousands of pages of public comment, much of it substantive, all of it now part of the administrative record. They are taught here as historical worked examples; for your own submission you will pick a currently open docket that overlaps your project, using the search workflow described below.

21.1 What a public comment is

The Administrative Procedure Act of 1946 is the legal scaffolding under which most federal regulation happens. Its most-cited provision for our purposes is 5 U.S.C. § 553, “Rule making,” which requires that when an agency proposes a substantive rule, it must publish notice in the Federal Register, give the public an opportunity to participate “through submission of written data, views, or arguments,” and consider what it receives before issuing a final rule (US Congress 1946). That sentence is short, but the obligations it creates are load-bearing. An agency that skips the public comment step, or that ignores comments raising a “significant” issue, risks having its rule vacated in court as “arbitrary and capricious.” The comment period is the mechanism by which federal rules are made legally durable.

The mechanics are standardized. An agency publishes an NPRM in the Federal Register. The NPRM names a docket number, describes the proposed rule, explains the agency’s legal authority, summarizes the evidence, and almost always asks the public a series of specific questions. It announces a comment period, typically 30 to 90 days. During that window, anyone, US citizen or not, may submit comments through regulations.gov or directly to the agency. At the close, the agency reviews comments, addresses significant issues in the preamble, and publishes the final rule.

Three features make comments worth your attention. They are written, which suits data scientists. They are on the agency’s clock, not yours. And the substantive ones become part of the administrative record: the bundle of documents a reviewing court will examine if the rule is later challenged. A comment you wrote in 2024 may be cited in a 2029 opinion. That is an unusual half-life for a weekend’s work.

21.2 Why data scientists underuse this genre

Comments are lower-friction than testimony and higher-leverage than op-eds, and the profession submits shockingly few. Three reasons recur.

First, nobody invited you. A congressional committee selects its witnesses; a newspaper commissions its op-eds. Rulemaking is the opposite: the agency publishes the NPRM, and the comment box sits waiting for whoever shows up. The lack of an invitation is the point. It is also, for a profession trained on peer review, psychologically disorienting.

Second, the form is unfamiliar. Agencies do not publish style guides for comments the way newspapers publish pitch guidelines. You have to figure out on your own that a substantive comment cites the NPRM’s section numbers, attaches supporting evidence, and ends with specific proposed changes. The conventions exist; they are just not written down where a student would find them.

Third, the outcome is uncertain. You submit, and the agency either adopts your suggestion, partially adopts it, ignores it, or rejects it in a paragraph of the final rule’s preamble. For a profession that likes metrics, a genre whose principal effect is cumulative can feel unrewarding. It is, nonetheless, how most federal regulation is shaped.

21.3 State and local equivalents

Federal rulemaking is the best-documented system, but not the only one. Colorado’s state rulemaking process runs through the Secretary of State’s office, which publishes the Colorado Register and maintains a rules-in-process portal at https://www.sos.state.co.us/CCR/Welcome.do. Colorado attorney general opinions, Public Utilities Commission dockets, and Department of Regulatory Agencies rulemakings all follow an analogous notice-and-comment rhythm. City governments run their own processes: Boulder City Council hearings accept written comment before meetings, Denver zoning amendments go through formal periods, Boulder Valley School District policy revisions allow written comment before the board votes. The mechanics differ. The genre is the same: a written, time-bounded, substantive argument submitted into a governmental record.

If federal rulemaking feels too abstract for your project, a state or city comment is a reasonable first artifact. The drafting discipline transfers, and the local audience is often more responsive.

21.4 The substantive comment standard

Agencies distinguish, informally but consistently, between substantive and form-letter comments. A substantive comment does at least three things: it identifies itself as responding to specific provisions of the NPRM (by section and paragraph), it offers evidence or analysis the agency did not already have, and it proposes specific changes to the rule. A form letter repeats, verbatim, text that a campaign asked supporters to paste into the comment box. Both are counted, but differently. The administrative record treats a thousand identical form letters as evidence of public sentiment; it treats one substantive comment as potentially dispositive evidence the agency must address in the final rule. Courts reviewing a rule for arbitrariness will look for whether the agency engaged with substantive comments. They will not expect the agency to engage, paragraph by paragraph, with a form-letter campaign (Congressional Research Service 2023).

This is good news for the public-interest data scientist. You cannot out-organize a thousand-member advocacy group at volume. You can produce one comment the agency is legally obliged to take seriously, which is a much better return on your weekend.

TipThe Missing Manual

“Comment flooding” is what happens when a campaign tool posts the same text a thousand times under a thousand names. Agencies treat identical or near-identical comments as one comment with a thousand signatories, not a thousand substantive arguments. This cuts both ways: your one carefully written comment is not drowned out, but if your comment looks like a flood entry (no section citations, no evidence, no specifics), it is processed accordingly regardless of your credentials. Separately, the regulations.gov API has changed endpoints and authentication mechanisms several times across the past decade. Before writing code against it, open https://open.gsa.gov/api/regulationsgov/ and confirm the current version matches what this chapter describes. Treat the API verification as part of Exercise 21.1; note divergences in your writeup.

21.5 The regulations.gov API

Regulations.gov is the shared federal portal where most agencies post dockets and collect comments. It exposes a REST API through api.data.gov, which requires a free API key you request by email (US General Services Administration 2024). The three endpoints you will use most are the document search (/v4/documents), the docket retrieval (/v4/dockets/{id}), and the comment submission endpoint (which has had different names across API versions; verify before writing against it).

The documentation has shifted. Older tutorials point at /api/v3/documents or at regulations.gov’s internal API, neither of which is the current canonical entry point. Treat the GSA open API documentation as authoritative and any code sample older than a year as provisional.

An illustrative search for open dockets matching a keyword:

# Verify endpoint and auth against https://open.gsa.gov/api/regulationsgov/
# before running. The API has changed at least twice since 2020.

import os
import requests

API_KEY = os.environ["REGULATIONS_GOV_API_KEY"]
BASE = "https://api.regulations.gov/v4"

HEADERS = {
    "X-Api-Key": API_KEY,
    "Accept": "application/vnd.api+json",
    "User-Agent": "PublicInterestDataScience/0.1 (your-email@example.edu)",
}

def search_dockets(keyword, page_size=25):
    params = {
        "filter[searchTerm]": keyword,
        "filter[documentType]": "Proposed Rule",
        "page[size]": page_size,
        "sort": "-postedDate",
    }
    r = requests.get(f"{BASE}/documents", headers=HEADERS,
                     params=params, timeout=30)
    r.raise_for_status()
    return r.json()["data"]

hits = search_dockets("algorithmic accountability")
for h in hits[:5]:
    attrs = h["attributes"]
    print(attrs["docketId"], attrs["title"][:70],
          attrs.get("commentEndDate", "no end date"))
# => FTC-2022-0053 Trade Regulation Rule on Commercial Surveillance ...
# => NIST-2023-0009 AI Risk Management Framework ...

The payload uses the JSON:API convention, nesting attributes under attributes and relationships under relationships. Read it once with json.dumps(..., indent=2) before writing parsing code against it.

21.6 Fetching and reading an NPRM

The NPRM is usually a long PDF with a parallel HTML page. The Federal Register publishes a structured XML version easier to parse than the PDF; the regulations.gov API exposes it via the document’s fileFormats attribute.

def fetch_document(document_id):
    r = requests.get(f"{BASE}/documents/{document_id}",
                     headers=HEADERS, timeout=30)
    r.raise_for_status()
    return r.json()["data"]

doc = fetch_document("FTC-2022-0053-0001")
formats = doc["attributes"].get("fileFormats", [])

An NPRM has a predictable structure: preamble, background on statutory authority, alternatives analysis, proposed regulatory text, and almost always a numbered list of questions the agency is asking the public to address. Those numbered questions are the most valuable part of the document for a commenter. If the agency asked a question and you answered it with evidence, your comment is prima facie substantive.

A routine to extract question paragraphs from the HTML version:

# Note: the Federal Register's HTML structure varies by document;
# adjust selectors after inspecting the specific docket.

from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
import re

def extract_questions(html_text):
    soup = BeautifulSoup(html_text, "html.parser")
    questions = []
    for p in soup.find_all(["p", "li"]):
        text = p.get_text(" ", strip=True)
        if re.search(r"\?\s*$", text) and len(text) > 40:
            questions.append(text)
    return questions

# Expect roughly 20-100 explicit questions in a substantive NPRM.
# The FTC commercial surveillance ANPRM asked 95 numbered questions.

Ninety-five questions is a lot. You are not obliged to answer all of them. You are asking which two or three overlap your evidence base, and answering those with the best argument you can muster.

21.7 Tracking the docket over time

A rule you are following will accumulate documents over months: the NPRM, comment-period extensions, supplemental notices, industry responses, occasionally a second NPRM. The SQLite tracker from Chapter 10 adapts here with a small extension: add a rulemakings table and a comments table alongside the CORA schema.

import sqlite3
from pathlib import Path

DB_PATH = Path("rulemaking_tracker.sqlite")

SCHEMA = """
CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS rulemakings (
    docket_id TEXT PRIMARY KEY,
    agency TEXT NOT NULL,
    title TEXT NOT NULL,
    posted_date TEXT,
    comment_end_date TEXT,
    status TEXT NOT NULL
        CHECK (status IN ('open','closed','final','withdrawn')),
    notes TEXT
);
CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS comments (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY,
    docket_id TEXT NOT NULL REFERENCES rulemakings(docket_id),
    submitter TEXT,
    date_submitted TEXT,
    substantive INTEGER,   -- 0 or 1
    url TEXT,
    notes TEXT
);
"""

with sqlite3.connect(DB_PATH) as conn:
    conn.executescript(SCHEMA)

Reuse the insert-and-query patterns from Chapter 10. A weekly cron that polls the docket, inserts new documents, and flags rules with comment_end_date within 14 days will stop you missing windows. Missing the window is the single most common failure mode for first-time commenters.

21.8 The shape of a substantive comment

A comment has a header, an introduction, a body organized by the sections of the NPRM it addresses, and a conclusion with specific proposed changes. The first page looks like this, roughly:

[Your name, affiliation, and contact information]
[Date]

Re: Comment on [Agency] [Docket Number]
    [Full title of the NPRM]
    [Federal Register citation, e.g., 87 Fed. Reg. 51,273]

Introduction

I submit this comment on the [Agency]'s notice of proposed rulemaking
("NPRM") concerning [topic]. I am a [researcher, data scientist,
student] whose work focuses on [area]. I write in my individual
capacity; the views expressed are my own and not those of my employer.

The introduction establishes standing, the sections you intend to address, and a one-paragraph summary of your position. The body then addresses specific questions:

Response to Question 12 ("How should the Commission define 'automated
decision system' for purposes of this rule?")

The Commission's proposed definition in Section 2(a)(4) excludes
systems that are "advisory" rather than dispositive. This exclusion is
too broad. [Evidence paragraph citing a public audit, a peer-reviewed
study, or a dataset.] I propose the following revised text:

  (a)(4) "Automated decision system" means any software, including
  machine-learning models, that is used to make or materially support
  a decision affecting a legally protected interest, whether the
  decision is final or advisory.

Three things that paragraph does: names the question, cites the section, proposes concrete redlined text. Agency staff can draft a revised section from that paragraph without interpolation. That is the standard to aim for.

The conclusion restates the proposed changes as a short numbered list and provides contact information for followup. Attach evidence as exhibits. A comment with four exhibits (an audit report, a dataset card, a letter of support, a bibliography) reads as materially different from one with none.

21.9 Coalition comments and individual voice

Many of the most influential comments on the mid-2020s AI rulemakings were coalition submissions: ACLU with EFF and Upturn; the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights with a dozen signatories; academic working groups coordinated through the AI Now Institute or the Center for Democracy & Technology. A coalition comment has institutional weight and authorial dilution. An individual comment has the opposite profile: thinner institutional authority, sharper voice.

Neither dominates. If your argument rests on a technical audit you conducted, your individual signature is load-bearing and you should sign it yourself. If your argument matches thirty other civil-society groups’ arguments, your signature adds to a coalition letter. A heuristic: if what you are saying has not been said in this exact form by any current coalition letter, submit it under your own name.

NoteIn the Public Interest

A public comment becomes part of the administrative record the moment the agency accepts it. That record is not an archive of opinions; it is the evidentiary base on which future litigation depends. When a rule is challenged in the D.C. Circuit, the court reviews the record for whether the agency adequately considered “significant” comments. A substantive comment you submit in 2026 may be cited in a 2030 opinion vacating a rule for failure to respond. This is oversight as Chapter 7 defined it: the authority-and-remedy pair wired into the legal architecture of Chapter 10. Your comment is not a speech act but a durable artifact that gives courts, journalists, and scholars a reason to look at what the agency did and did not consider. Write it so it reads well out of context six years from now, because that is when it will be read.

21.10 A note on timing and the regulatory calendar

The comment period is non-negotiable. A comment filed after the window closes is, as a legal matter, absent from the record. Watch the close date. Watch the Federal Register for extension notices (agencies sometimes extend after pressure, occasionally by weeks). Watch for supplemental NPRMs that reopen comment on a narrower issue. ProPublica and the Administrative Conference of the United States maintain rulemaking trackers that surface the high-impact dockets without you scanning the Federal Register daily.

If you are drafting for a live federal docket, verify the docket is still open before you start. Confirm your chosen docket has not closed, been withdrawn, or moved to final rule. The FTC commercial surveillance docket and the NIST AI RMF cycles cited here are taught as historical worked examples from the 2022-2024 period; they are no longer accepting comments. Your instructor will help you pick a current replacement on a topic that overlaps your project.

21.12 Looking ahead

You now have, once Exercise 21.3 is drafted, the fifth of six Part V artifacts: a substantive comment on a live rulemaking, cited to section numbers, with concrete proposed text. Chapter 22 closes Part V and the book. The op-ed, the report, the proposal, the testimony, and the comment are durable only if someone can find them after your employer’s website is reorganized, your university decommissions your faculty page, and the agency moves its docket to an archival system nobody remembers how to search. Chapter 22 teaches you to deposit each artifact into archival infrastructure (the Internet Archive and Zenodo) with resolvable identifiers, metadata, and a plan for the next decade. It is the final step in turning a portfolio of work into a public record.

```python
    "User-Agent": "PublicInterestDataScience/0.1 (your-email@example.edu)",
}

def search_dockets(keyword, page_size=25):
    params = {
        "filter[searchTerm]": keyword,
        "filter[documentType]": "Proposed Rule",
        "page[size]": page_size,
        "sort": "-postedDate",
    }
    r = requests.get(f"{BASE}/documents", headers=HEADERS,
                     params=params, timeout=30)
    r.raise_for_status()
    return r.json()["data"]

hits = search_dockets("algorithmic accountability")
for h in hits[:5]:
    attrs = h["attributes"]
    print(attrs["docketId"], attrs["title"][:70],
          attrs.get("commentEndDate", "no end date"))
# => FTC-2022-0053 Trade Regulation Rule on Commercial Surveillance ...
# => NIST-2023-0009 AI Risk Management Framework ...
```

The payload uses the JSON:API convention, nesting attributes under `attributes` and relationships under `relationships`. Read it once with `json.dumps(..., indent=2)` before writing parsing code against it.

## Fetching and reading an NPRM

The NPRM is usually a long PDF with a parallel HTML page. The Federal Register publishes a structured XML version easier to parse than the PDF; the regulations.gov API exposes it via the document's `fileFormats` attribute.

```python
def fetch_document(document_id):
    r = requests.get(f"{BASE}/documents/{document_id}",
                     headers=HEADERS, timeout=30)
    r.raise_for_status()
    return r.json()["data"]

doc = fetch_document("FTC-2022-0053-0001")
formats = doc["attributes"].get("fileFormats", [])
```

An NPRM has a predictable structure: preamble, background on statutory authority, alternatives analysis, proposed regulatory text, and almost always a numbered list of questions the agency is asking the public to address. Those numbered questions are the most valuable part of the document for a commenter. If the agency asked a question and you answered it with evidence, your comment is prima facie substantive.

A routine to extract question paragraphs from the HTML version:

```python
# Note: the Federal Register's HTML structure varies by document;
# adjust selectors after inspecting the specific docket.

from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
import re

def extract_questions(html_text):
    soup = BeautifulSoup(html_text, "html.parser")
    questions = []
    for p in soup.find_all(["p", "li"]):
        text = p.get_text(" ", strip=True)
        if re.search(r"\?\s*$", text) and len(text) > 40:
            questions.append(text)
    return questions

# Expect roughly 20-100 explicit questions in a substantive NPRM.
# The FTC commercial surveillance ANPRM asked 95 numbered questions.
```

Ninety-five questions is a lot. You are not obliged to answer all of them. You are asking which two or three overlap your evidence base, and answering those with the best argument you can muster.

## Tracking the docket over time

A rule you are following will accumulate documents over months: the NPRM, comment-period extensions, supplemental notices, industry responses, occasionally a second NPRM. The SQLite tracker from @sec-ch-10-law adapts here with a small extension: add a `rulemakings` table and a `comments` table alongside the CORA schema.

```python
import sqlite3
from pathlib import Path

DB_PATH = Path("rulemaking_tracker.sqlite")

SCHEMA = """
CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS rulemakings (
    docket_id TEXT PRIMARY KEY,
    agency TEXT NOT NULL,
    title TEXT NOT NULL,
    posted_date TEXT,
    comment_end_date TEXT,
    status TEXT NOT NULL
        CHECK (status IN ('open','closed','final','withdrawn')),
    notes TEXT
);
CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS comments (
    id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY,
    docket_id TEXT NOT NULL REFERENCES rulemakings(docket_id),
    submitter TEXT,
    date_submitted TEXT,
    substantive INTEGER,   -- 0 or 1
    url TEXT,
    notes TEXT
);
"""

with sqlite3.connect(DB_PATH) as conn:
    conn.executescript(SCHEMA)
```

Reuse the insert-and-query patterns from Chapter 10. A weekly cron that polls the docket, inserts new documents, and flags rules with `comment_end_date` within 14 days will stop you missing windows. Missing the window is the single most common failure mode for first-time commenters.

## The shape of a substantive comment

A comment has a header, an introduction, a body organized by the sections of the NPRM it addresses, and a conclusion with specific proposed changes. The first page looks like this, roughly:

```
[Your name, affiliation, and contact information]
[Date]

Re: Comment on [Agency] [Docket Number]
    [Full title of the NPRM]
    [Federal Register citation, e.g., 87 Fed. Reg. 51,273]

Introduction

I submit this comment on the [Agency]'s notice of proposed rulemaking
("NPRM") concerning [topic]. I am a [researcher, data scientist,
student] whose work focuses on [area]. I write in my individual
capacity; the views expressed are my own and not those of my employer.
```

The introduction establishes standing, the sections you intend to address, and a one-paragraph summary of your position. The body then addresses specific questions:

```
Response to Question 12 ("How should the Commission define 'automated
decision system' for purposes of this rule?")

The Commission's proposed definition in Section 2(a)(4) excludes
systems that are "advisory" rather than dispositive. This exclusion is
too broad. [Evidence paragraph citing a public audit, a peer-reviewed
study, or a dataset.] I propose the following revised text:

  (a)(4) "Automated decision system" means any software, including
  machine-learning models, that is used to make or materially support
  a decision affecting a legally protected interest, whether the
  decision is final or advisory.
```

Three things that paragraph does: names the question, cites the section, proposes concrete redlined text. Agency staff can draft a revised section from that paragraph without interpolation. That is the standard to aim for.

The conclusion restates the proposed changes as a short numbered list and provides contact information for followup. Attach evidence as exhibits. A comment with four exhibits (an audit report, a dataset card, a letter of support, a bibliography) reads as materially different from one with none.

## Coalition comments and individual voice

Many of the most influential comments on the mid-2020s AI rulemakings were coalition submissions: ACLU with EFF and Upturn; the Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights with a dozen signatories; academic working groups coordinated through the AI Now Institute or the Center for Democracy & Technology. A coalition comment has institutional weight and authorial dilution. An individual comment has the opposite profile: thinner institutional authority, sharper voice.

Neither dominates. If your argument rests on a technical audit you conducted, your individual signature is load-bearing and you should sign it yourself. If your argument matches thirty other civil-society groups' arguments, your signature adds to a coalition letter. A heuristic: if what you are saying has not been said in this exact form by any current coalition letter, submit it under your own name.

::: {.callout-note title="In the Public Interest"}
A public comment becomes part of the administrative record the moment the agency accepts it. That record is not an archive of opinions; it is the evidentiary base on which future litigation depends. When a rule is challenged in the D.C. Circuit, the court reviews the record for whether the agency adequately considered "significant" comments. A substantive comment you submit in 2026 may be cited in a 2030 opinion vacating a rule for failure to respond. This is oversight as @sec-ch-07-oversight defined it: the authority-and-remedy pair wired into the legal architecture of @sec-ch-10-law. Your comment is not a speech act but a durable artifact that gives courts, journalists, and scholars a reason to look at what the agency did and did not consider. Write it so it reads well out of context six years from now, because that is when it will be read.
:::

## A note on timing and the regulatory calendar

The comment period is non-negotiable. A comment filed after the window closes is, as a legal matter, absent from the record. Watch the close date. Watch the Federal Register for extension notices (agencies sometimes extend after pressure, occasionally by weeks). Watch for supplemental NPRMs that reopen comment on a narrower issue. ProPublica and the Administrative Conference of the United States maintain rulemaking trackers that surface the high-impact dockets without you scanning the Federal Register daily.

If you are drafting for a live federal docket, verify the docket is still open before you start. Confirm your chosen docket has not closed, been withdrawn, or moved to final rule. The FTC commercial surveillance docket and the NIST AI RMF cycles cited here are taught as historical worked examples from the 2022-2024 period; they are no longer accepting comments. Your instructor will help you pick a current replacement on a topic that overlaps your project.

ACUS publishes recommendations and research on rulemaking practice; a useful gateway to current scholarship on which rules merit close attention.
