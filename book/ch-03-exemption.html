4  Exemption: Who Must Answer?

5 Exemption: Who Must Answer?

Chapter 2 described a world in which what a researcher can observe is shrinking. This chapter is about a related but distinct problem. Suppose you can observe the outputs of a system. They are already on a published spreadsheet, or in a press release, or scrolling through a caseworker’s terminal. Who is obliged to explain how they were produced? Who has to answer when they go wrong? In the United States in the mid-2020s, the honest answer for a remarkable share of consequential automated decisions is: no one with enforceable authority, on any clock a parent or a journalist or a state legislator can use.

Enclosure asks who can observe? Exemption asks who must answer? Enclosure operates through the terms of access to data. Exemption operates through trade-secret law, procurement contracts, qualified immunity, sovereign immunity, statutory carve-outs from freedom-of-information regimes, and the simple fact that many public agencies run proprietary software they did not build and cannot fully inspect. Pasquale (2016) named this the black box society: a condition in which the scoring and sorting machinery that organizes credit, insurance, hiring, and policing is legally and practically sealed off from the people it sorts. A decade later, the black box has migrated further into the machinery of the state itself. When the exempt actor is a commercial platform, you can at least pretend to buy your way in or shame your way in. When the exempt actor is the state, using a vendor’s system to decide who gets a benefit, the pretense collapses.

This chapter takes up two cases. The long example is the Allegheny Family Screening Tool (AFST), a risk-scoring system used by the Allegheny County, Pennsylvania child-welfare hotline since 2016. AFST is unusually well-documented for a government-deployed risk score, and you will build an evidence ledger for it from public sources. The short comparison is the Dutch state’s Systeem Risico Indicatie (SyRI) and the childcare-benefits fraud detection system that prompted the toeslagenaffaire scandal. The Dutch cases matter because they are the rare examples in which a national court found an automated decision system unlawful and shut it down (District Court of The Hague 2020). Reading the U.S. case against the Dutch one tells you which claims about opacity have purchase, and in which legal climates.

5.1 Two kinds of exemption

Separate legal exemption from practical exemption before you go near a keyboard.

Legal exemption is the carved-out space where disclosure obligations do not reach. Trade-secret protections under the Defend Trade Secrets Act and state analogues travel with a vendor into a public agency’s procurement file. FOIA exemptions 4 (trade secrets) and 7 (law enforcement) are routinely invoked to withhold model specifications, training data, and performance audits. The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) historically chilled independent testing: until Sandvig v. Barr (American Civil Liberties Union 2019), academic auditors faced credible threat of federal criminal prosecution for creating test accounts that violated a platform’s terms. Sandvig narrowed that risk for academic research but did not eliminate civil exposure and did not reach state-level computer crime laws. Terms-of-service bindings tie back to Chapter 4: what the vendor’s lawyers wrote at procurement time continues to govern what the public can ask later.

Practical exemption is the larger space. Even where the law would require disclosure, the route is slow, fragmented, and under-enforced. A reporter who files a state open-records request for a vendor contract waits six months and receives a PDF with most of its text redacted. A parent whose family was screened out of services reads a denial letter citing an “assessment” without naming the model, the version, the features, or the threshold. The information exists. Nobody is obliged to aggregate it. Exemption does not require secrecy. It only requires that the cost of reconstructing the picture exceed what a citizen, a reporter, or an underfunded NGO can afford. Fragmentation is the weapon.

NoteIn the Public Interest

Oversight, as Chapter 9 will develop it, is not a property of a single document. It is a property of the linkage between documents: a procurement contract that names a vendor, a model card that describes that vendor’s system, a FOIA response that discloses subgroup performance, a court filing that tests a denied claim. Exemption thrives on fragmentation because each artifact taken alone is plausible. The contract does not mention the model by name. The model card does not mention the contract. The denial letter mentions neither. Keegan (2026) calls this the linkability element of the installed base, and without it, oversight is a collection of disconnected complaints. Public-interest data science is, in part, the patient reconstruction of linkages the system was designed to leave broken.

5.2 Allegheny Family Screening Tool, as a public object

Allegheny County’s Office of Children, Youth and Families receives roughly fifteen thousand child-welfare referrals a year. Since August 2016, hotline call screeners have seen, alongside the case narrative, a risk score produced by the Allegheny Family Screening Tool. The score estimates the probability that a child named in the referral will be re-referred, removed from the home, or placed in foster care within a defined window. A screener uses the score as one input in deciding whether to open a formal investigation.

Eubanks (2018) treated AFST in Automating Inequality as a paradigmatic case of a predictive instrument that extends state surveillance into poor families under the rubric of decision support. The county and the research team have disputed some characterizations and affirmed others. AFST is unusual in that a reader who wants to know how the system works can assemble a nontrivial picture from public sources. It is also typical in that the picture is incomplete, and the incompleteness is not random.

5.3 The evidence ledger

The technique is deliberately unglamorous. Read every public document you can find about the system. For each, record what it tells you and, just as importantly, what it does not. The pandas DataFrame below is an evidence ledger: a structured record of provenance that makes gaps visible on the same footing as findings.

import pandas as pd

rows = [
    {
        "source": "Allegheny County RFP and vendor selection memo",
        "date": "2014-11", "type": "procurement",
        "what_it_tells_us": "Names Auckland University of Technology's "
            "Centre for Social Data Analytics as the selected team; "
            "lists the data systems to be integrated.",
        "what_it_doesnt": "Does not disclose the price, the model "
            "family, or the performance targets the vendor was "
            "expected to meet.",
    },
    {
        "source": "Vaithianathan et al. methodology report",
        "date": "2019-04", "type": "technical_report",
        "what_it_tells_us": "Describes feature engineering on 131 "
            "variables from the county data warehouse; reports AUC "
            "and calibration on historical data.",
        "what_it_doesnt": "Does not disclose the production decision "
            "threshold, the retraining cadence, or subgroup "
            "performance on race and ethnicity as deployed.",
    },
    {
        "source": "Chouldechova et al., FAccT 2018",
        "date": "2018-02", "type": "peer_reviewed",
        "what_it_tells_us": "Subgroup accuracy and error-rate "
            "disparity analysis on a research version of the model.",
        "what_it_doesnt": "Research version is not identical to the "
            "fielded tool; ground-truth labels are proxies, which "
            "the authors acknowledge.",
    },
    {
        "source": "Eubanks, Automating Inequality, ch. 4",
        "date": "2018-01", "type": "book",
        "what_it_tells_us": "Situates AFST within the county's "
            "broader data integration project; documents community "
            "concerns and the surveillance/referral feedback loop.",
        "what_it_doesnt": "Cannot speak to model changes after 2017 "
            "fieldwork; the tool has been revised multiple times.",
    },
    {
        "source": "Associated Press / Ho and Burke",
        "date": "2022-04", "type": "press",
        "what_it_tells_us": "Reports DOJ civil-rights inquiry into "
            "disability-based discrimination in the tool's deployment.",
        "what_it_doesnt": "DOJ inquiry materials are not public; we "
            "learn an inquiry exists but not what it found.",
    },

evidence = pd.DataFrame(rows)
evidence["date"] = pd.to_datetime(evidence["date"])
evidence = evidence.sort_values("date").reset_index(drop=True)
# => 5 rows, columns: source, date, type, what_it_tells_us, what_it_doesnt

gaps = evidence[["date", "source", "what_it_doesnt"]]
gaps.to_csv("afst_gaps.csv", index=False)

Five rows is not a lot. That is the point. A mature ledger for a deployed municipal system might contain thirty or forty rows, organized by procurement, technical documentation, peer review, press, litigation, and community record. The two columns matter. what_it_tells_us is the affirmative claim you can source-cite. what_it_doesnt is the negative space that tells you what you would still need to file, subpoena, or discover in order to make a stronger claim. A one-column ledger is a bibliography. A two-column ledger is the beginning of an accountability argument.

Two observations follow. The most technically detailed document (Vaithianathan and colleagues’ methodology report (Vaithianathan et al. 2019)) is useful for understanding the research-time model but silent on the deployed threshold and retraining schedule. The most politically charged finding (the DOJ civil-rights inquiry reported by Ho and Burke (2022)) is the one where the underlying record is least available: we know an inquiry opened, not what it concluded.

TipThe Missing Manual

The model card does not say. The methodology report does not say. The procurement memo does not say. Those three silences are themselves data. A standard failure mode of algorithmic-accountability writing is to treat the absence as a rhetorical flourish (“notably, the county does not disclose…”) and move on. Do not move on. Write the absence into the ledger as an object with a date, a source, and a what_it_doesnt entry. You will use it later when you file a records request, when you testify, or when you draft a rulemaking comment. An absence that has a citation is an absence you can litigate. An absence you only gestured at in prose is a complaint.

5.4 Reading the deployed model from the outside

The ledger tells you what documentation exists. It does not tell you how the model is behaving today. For a deployed system, three kinds of external inference are available without breaking any law.

The first is decision-record inspection. Some jurisdictions, including Allegheny County at various points, release aggregate screening statistics: calls screened in, screened out, and referred, broken down by some subset of demographic variables. Those aggregates let you compute, crudely, whether the score’s introduction shifted selection rates overall. They do not let you recover individual decisions.

The second is disparate-impact analysis on outcomes. If you can obtain downstream placement data (via FOIA, a collaborator inside the agency, academic partnership), you can estimate whether scored referrals have different outcomes by race, age, or disability than unscored referrals. The Chouldechova and colleagues FAccT paper (Chouldechova et al. 2018) is an example of this kind of work on a pre-deployment version.

The third is audit-by-adversarial-use: submitting test cases through the live interface. CFAA exposure still matters here. You would need counsel’s sign-off, institutional cover, and a clear understanding of which Sandvig protections reach your facts. Most municipal child-welfare systems do not expose an API you could query, which is why audit-by-use is rare in this domain and common in hiring and advertising contexts.

# Illustrative skeleton only. Do not run against a live agency system.
def compute_selection_rates(decisions):
    """Aggregate screen-in rates by demographic group.

    Input: one row per referral with columns
        {referral_id, group, score, screened_in}.
    """
    g = decisions.groupby("group").agg(
        n=("referral_id", "count"),
        screened_in=("screened_in", "sum"),
    )
    g["rate"] = g["screened_in"] / g["n"]
    return g.reset_index()

# Expected output columns: group, n, screened_in, rate

None of this code is the audit. The code is the scaffolding you build so that, when a data-sharing agreement is in place or an agency publishes aggregates, you have somewhere to put the numbers. The substantive work is upstream: negotiating the sharing agreement, filing the records request, obtaining IRB approval. Chapter 12 takes up the legal scaffolding in detail.

5.5 When the state is the adversary: SyRI and after

The Dutch case inverts a habit U.S. readers bring to these problems. In the United States, the usual adversary in an algorithmic accountability story is a commercial vendor whose system has been adopted by a public agency. The vendor holds the trade-secret claim; the agency carries the disclosure obligation; advocates play them against each other. In SyRI, the adversary was the state itself.

Systeem Risico Indicatie was a cross-agency data-integration tool that scored households for fraud risk across welfare, taxation, labor, and housing data. It was deployed in targeted neighborhoods beginning in 2014. In February 2020, the District Court of The Hague ruled that SyRI violated Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights because the state’s interest in fraud detection did not justify the opacity and breadth of the system (District Court of The Hague 2020). The ruling did not turn on the accuracy of the model. It turned on the state’s failure to make the system legible enough for citizens to understand and challenge how they were being assessed. Dutch scholars reading the aftermath (Arruda and van Schendel 2024) note that successor systems have adapted the legal wrapper without fully abandoning the underlying practice, and that the toeslagenaffaire childcare-benefits scandal exposed a parallel, more damaging pattern in the tax administration.

Read the childcare-benefits scandal alongside AFST. Amnesty International’s report (Amnesty International 2021) documents how the Dutch tax authority used a risk-classification model that flagged dual-nationality applicants at elevated rates, triggering aggressive clawbacks that pushed tens of thousands of families into debt and, in many cases, into losing their children to state care. The Rutte cabinet eventually resigned over it. Opacity was not incidental to the harm: families could not learn why they had been flagged, which made it impossible to contest individual decisions or to surface the pattern until journalism and parliamentary inquiry reconstructed it from outside (Geiger and Braun 2023).

Three contrasts with AFST matter. First, the Dutch regime provided a forum (the Article 8 claim) in which system-level opacity could be litigated directly, rather than through the U.S. habit of challenging individual denials. Second, the affected class was organized enough, through civil-society groups and parliamentary allies, to press the claim to a national court. Third, the remedy was shutdown, not disclosure: the court did not order SyRI to open its model. It ordered the state to stop running it. Chapter 12 asks under what U.S. doctrinal conditions an equivalent remedy would be available, and the answer is: under very few, and none at the federal level as of this writing.

TipThe Missing Manual

When the state is the exempt actor, strategies that assume the state is a neutral arbiter between citizen and vendor stop working. Most algorithmic-accountability tooling (model cards, datasheets for datasets, fairness audits) is designed for a world in which the state is the party demanding accountability from a vendor. In the SyRI and toeslagenaffaire cases, the party deploying the system, the party holding the data, and the party refusing disclosure were all the same ministry. The governance textbook does not cover that case, because the governance textbook was written by people employed by the ministry. Notice when you are inside that scenario, and notice that your methods have to change.

5.6 Practical exemption as a design choice

Exemption is not fate. It is a set of design and procurement choices that can be made differently. The European Union’s 2024 AI Act, New York City’s Local Law 144, and a growing list of state-level algorithmic-accountability bills are attempts to legislate a floor under transparency. Whether any produces working oversight depends on the same six elements Chapter 7 will name: linkable records, interpretable categories, persistent evidence, safe scrutiny, authority that can act, and remedy that reaches the affected. A statute that requires a “model card” without specifying linkage to deployment records produces compliance artifacts and no oversight.

The public-interest response to exemption has two parts. The first is the patient work this chapter has rehearsed: building evidence ledgers that make absences visible, linking artifacts across silos, naming the gaps in terms a lawyer or a legislator can use. The second is designing forward: when you have authority to shape a procurement, a contract, or a rulemaking, writing linkability in as a requirement rather than a nice-to-have. Chapter 9 and Chapter 24 develop both moves.

5.8 Looking ahead

Enclosure takes data out of reach. Exemption keeps the people and institutions that use data out of reach. Chapter 4 takes up the third pressure in Part I: erosion. Even when data is accessible and decision-makers are nominally accountable, the record of what happened can dissolve. Databases are reorganized without migration paths. Web pages are silently edited. Legacy systems are decommissioned faster than their contents can be archived. If enclosure asks who can observe? and exemption asks who must answer?, erosion asks what can be remembered?

Five rows is not a lot. That is the point. A mature ledger for a deployed municipal system might contain thirty or forty rows, organized by procurement, technical documentation, peer review, press, litigation, and community record. The two columns matter. `what_it_tells_us` is the affirmative claim you can source-cite. `what_it_doesnt` is the negative space that tells you what you would still need to file, subpoena, or discover in order to make a stronger claim. A one-column ledger is a bibliography. A two-column ledger is the beginning of an accountability argument.

Two observations follow. The most technically detailed document (Vaithianathan and colleagues' methodology report [@vaithianathan2019]) is useful for understanding the research-time model but silent on the deployed threshold and retraining schedule. The most politically charged finding (the DOJ civil-rights inquiry reported by Ho and Burke [-@ho2022]) is the one where the underlying record is least available: we know an inquiry opened, not what it concluded.

::: {.callout-tip title="The Missing Manual"}
The model card does not say. The methodology report does not say. The procurement memo does not say. Those three silences are themselves data. A standard failure mode of algorithmic-accountability writing is to treat the absence as a rhetorical flourish ("notably, the county does not disclose...") and move on. Do not move on. Write the absence into the ledger as an object with a date, a source, and a `what_it_doesnt` entry. You will use it later when you file a records request, when you testify, or when you draft a rulemaking comment. An absence that has a citation is an absence you can litigate. An absence you only gestured at in prose is a complaint.
:::

## Reading the deployed model from the outside

The ledger tells you what documentation exists. It does not tell you how the model is behaving today. For a deployed system, three kinds of external inference are available without breaking any law.

The first is **decision-record inspection**. Some jurisdictions, including Allegheny County at various points, release aggregate screening statistics: calls screened in, screened out, and referred, broken down by some subset of demographic variables. Those aggregates let you compute, crudely, whether the score's introduction shifted selection rates overall. They do not let you recover individual decisions.

The second is **disparate-impact analysis on outcomes**. If you can obtain downstream placement data (via FOIA, a collaborator inside the agency, academic partnership), you can estimate whether scored referrals have different outcomes by race, age, or disability than unscored referrals. The Chouldechova and colleagues FAccT paper [@chouldechova2018] is an example of this kind of work on a pre-deployment version.

The third is **audit-by-adversarial-use**: submitting test cases through the live interface. CFAA exposure still matters here. You would need counsel's sign-off, institutional cover, and a clear understanding of which *Sandvig* protections reach your facts. Most municipal child-welfare systems do not expose an API you could query, which is why audit-by-use is rare in this domain and common in hiring and advertising contexts.

```python
# Illustrative skeleton only. Do not run against a live agency system.
def compute_selection_rates(decisions):
    """Aggregate screen-in rates by demographic group.

    Input: one row per referral with columns
        {referral_id, group, score, screened_in}.
    """
    g = decisions.groupby("group").agg(
        n=("referral_id", "count"),
        screened_in=("screened_in", "sum"),
    )
    g["rate"] = g["screened_in"] / g["n"]
    return g.reset_index()

# Expected output columns: group, n, screened_in, rate
```

None of this code is the audit. The code is the scaffolding you build so that, when a data-sharing agreement is in place or an agency publishes aggregates, you have somewhere to put the numbers. The substantive work is upstream: negotiating the sharing agreement, filing the records request, obtaining IRB approval. @sec-ch-10-law takes up the legal scaffolding in detail.

## When the state is the adversary: SyRI and after

The Dutch case inverts a habit U.S. readers bring to these problems. In the United States, the usual adversary in an algorithmic accountability story is a commercial vendor whose system has been adopted by a public agency. The vendor holds the trade-secret claim; the agency carries the disclosure obligation; advocates play them against each other. In SyRI, the adversary was the state itself.

Systeem Risico Indicatie was a cross-agency data-integration tool that scored households for fraud risk across welfare, taxation, labor, and housing data. It was deployed in targeted neighborhoods beginning in 2014. In February 2020, the District Court of The Hague ruled that SyRI violated Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights because the state's interest in fraud detection did not justify the opacity and breadth of the system [@syri2020]. The ruling did not turn on the accuracy of the model. It turned on the state's failure to make the system legible enough for citizens to understand and challenge how they were being assessed. Dutch scholars reading the aftermath [@deArruda2024] note that successor systems have adapted the legal wrapper without fully abandoning the underlying practice, and that the *toeslagenaffaire* childcare-benefits scandal exposed a parallel, more damaging pattern in the tax administration.

Read the childcare-benefits scandal alongside AFST. Amnesty International's report [@amnesty2021] documents how the Dutch tax authority used a risk-classification model that flagged dual-nationality applicants at elevated rates, triggering aggressive clawbacks that pushed tens of thousands of families into debt and, in many cases, into losing their children to state care. The Rutte cabinet eventually resigned over it. Opacity was not incidental to the harm: families could not learn why they had been flagged, which made it impossible to contest individual decisions or to surface the pattern until journalism and parliamentary inquiry reconstructed it from outside [@rotterdam_lighthouse2023].

Three contrasts with AFST matter. First, the Dutch regime provided a forum (the Article 8 claim) in which system-level opacity could be litigated directly, rather than through the U.S. habit of challenging individual denials. Second, the affected class was organized enough, through civil-society groups and parliamentary allies, to press the claim to a national court. Third, the remedy was shutdown, not disclosure: the court did not order SyRI to open its model. It ordered the state to stop running it. @sec-ch-10-law asks under what U.S. doctrinal conditions an equivalent remedy would be available, and the answer is: under very few, and none at the federal level as of this writing.

::: {.callout-tip title="The Missing Manual"}
When the state is the exempt actor, strategies that assume the state is a neutral arbiter between citizen and vendor stop working. Most algorithmic-accountability tooling (model cards, datasheets for datasets, fairness audits) is designed for a world in which the state is the party demanding accountability from a vendor. In the SyRI and *toeslagenaffaire* cases, the party deploying the system, the party holding the data, and the party refusing disclosure were all the same ministry. The governance textbook does not cover that case, because the governance textbook was written by people employed by the ministry. Notice when you are inside that scenario, and notice that your methods have to change.
:::

## Practical exemption as a design choice

Exemption is not fate. It is a set of design and procurement choices that can be made differently. The European Union's 2024 AI Act, New York City's Local Law 144, and a growing list of state-level algorithmic-accountability bills are attempts to legislate a floor under transparency. Whether any produces working oversight depends on the same six elements @sec-ch-05-installed-base will name: linkable records, interpretable categories, persistent evidence, safe scrutiny, authority that can act, and remedy that reaches the affected. A statute that requires a "model card" without specifying linkage to deployment records produces compliance artifacts and no oversight.

The public-interest response to exemption has two parts. The first is the patient work this chapter has rehearsed: building evidence ledgers that make absences visible, linking artifacts across silos, naming the gaps in terms a lawyer or a legislator can use. The second is designing forward: when you have authority to shape a procurement, a contract, or a rulemaking, writing linkability in as a requirement rather than a nice-to-have. @sec-ch-07-oversight and @sec-ch-22-archival-deposits develop both moves.

## Looking ahead

Enclosure takes data out of reach. Exemption keeps the people and institutions that use data out of reach. Chapter 4 takes up the third pressure in Part I: **erosion**. Even when data is accessible and decision-makers are nominally accountable, the record of what happened can dissolve. Databases are reorganized without migration paths. Web pages are silently edited. Legacy systems are decommissioned faster than their contents can be archived. If enclosure asks *who can observe?* and exemption asks *who must answer?*, erosion asks *what can be remembered?*
