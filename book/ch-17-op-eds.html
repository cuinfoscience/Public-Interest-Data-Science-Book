19 Op-Eds

Parts I through IV have been about learning to see. You have traced the structural pressures that constrain public knowledge (Chapter 4, Chapter 5, Chapter 6), named the countervailing values that hold accountability together (Chapter 8, Chapter 9, Chapter 10), studied the professional lineages that have done public-interest work for longer than data science has existed (Chapter 11 through Chapter 15), and triangulated your practice against the fields it is not (Chapter 16, Chapter 17, Chapter 18). That work has produced a diagnosis and a vocabulary. It has not yet produced a reader.

Part V is the pivot from seeing to doing. Each of the next six chapters takes a distinct genre of public writing or making, traces its history and its audience, and walks you through producing a real artifact in that form. By the end of Part V you will have, on disk and ready to submit, an op-ed, a report, a research proposal, a testimony statement, a public comment on a live rulemaking, and an archival deposit with a resolvable identifier. The chapters are independent enough that you can read them out of order, but the artifact convention is not optional. The chapter is not done when you have read it; the chapter is done when you have sent the draft. This one starts with the shortest form and the widest audience.

19.1 Why the op-ed matters, and why it does not

The op-ed is the 800-word argument you write for a stranger. It is not a report (Chapter 20), which assumes a policy audience willing to read 40 pages. It is not testimony (Chapter 22), which assumes a subpoena-strength reason for you to be at the microphone. It is a piece of persuasion, addressed to a general reader who has approximately four minutes and two competing browser tabs, written in the voice of a person who thinks something should happen. Most of what you have built in previous chapters (the audit, the dataset, the scraper) is illegible to that reader. The op-ed is what makes it legible.

It is also the genre public-interest data scientists most frequently skip. The report goes to the funder. The paper goes to the committee. The dashboard goes to the client. None of them reach the person whose benefits were denied, the council member whose zoning vote is next Tuesday, or the reporter who might pick up the thread if they knew the thread existed. An op-ed that is published in a regional paper is read by more members of the affected public than any peer-reviewed article you will write this year. That is an uncomfortable fact, and one the profession has mostly refused to sit with.

19.2 A short history of a strange page

The op-ed page (literally, the page opposite the editorial) is younger than most of the datasets you work with. The New York Times ran its first recognizable op-ed page on September 21, 1970, under editorial page editor John B. Oakes and op-ed editor Harrison Salisbury (Oakes 1970). The idea was to make room for outside voices on the page facing the paper’s unsigned institutional editorials: voices that did not agree with the Times, that came from outside journalism, and that addressed the day’s questions with a specificity the editorial page was too institutional to risk.

The op-ed page spread from the Times to other broadsheets through the 1970s and 1980s, and with it a set of conventions that still govern most pitches today: roughly 750 words, an argument pegged to the news cycle, a voice the paper’s own columnists cannot reliably produce. In 2021, the Times renamed the form “guest essays” on the grounds that the page-layout metaphor no longer matched the digital product (Kingsbury 2021). Most outlets have quietly followed, though the genre itself is stable. Writers still call them op-eds.

What has changed since 1970 is the ecosystem in which the op-ed sits. The legacy broadsheets still publish op-eds, but their slush piles are brutal and their turnaround on unsolicited pitches is measured in weeks. Around them has grown a second tier that is, for most public-interest data scientists, more realistic and in some ways more useful: The Conversation US, which commissions op-eds from academic authors and republishes them under Creative Commons to hundreds of regional outlets; Inside Higher Ed and The Chronicle of Higher Education, which publish arguments on research policy; MIT Technology Review and Wired, which publish longer technology op-eds with reporting attached; regional dailies like the Boulder Daily Camera and the Colorado Sun, whose readers include the city councils and county commissioners your audit is actually addressed to; and the newsletter tier (Substack, Ghost, individual blogs) where you publish the same 800 words to a self-selected audience with zero gatekeeping.

19.3 Who gets published where

The op-ed page is not a neutral meritocracy. The Op-Ed Project, a nonprofit founded by Catherine Orenstein in 2008, has for fifteen years documented the demographic distribution of bylines in major U.S. op-ed pages. Their byline surveys consistently find that roughly 80 percent of opinion writers in the legacy broadsheets are men, and that writers of color are underrepresented at ratios that have improved slowly and unevenly since tracking began (The Op-Ed Project 2022, 2024). The explanation is not that underrepresented writers pitch less often, though they do; it is that the pitch economy runs on weak-tie networks (Rolodexes, conference circuits, editor DMs) and those networks are the ones the profession has always had. If you are reading this book, you have reasons to pitch that are exactly as legitimate as anyone else’s. Pitch anyway.

The political economy also shapes what counts as an “op-ed-able” argument. A piece that says a city’s predictive-policing vendor is overfitting to historical arrests is op-ed-able; a piece that says policing is itself the problem is, at most outlets, not. The constraint is not a conspiracy. It is a product of what editors believe their median reader will finish. You will do better work if you understand the constraint, write around it where you can, and name it when you cannot.

19.4 The anatomy of an 800-word argument

An op-ed is not a short paper. It is an argument with a particular load-bearing structure, and editors can diagnose whether the structure is there within sixty seconds of opening your attachment. Five parts:

The hook is the first sentence, or at most the first two. It is the news peg, the anecdote, the surprising number. Its job is to earn the next paragraph.

The nut graf (from journalism; the paragraph that says “in a nutshell”) is where you tell the reader what your argument is. Not what the piece is about. What you want the reader to believe by the end. If you cannot write your nut graf as a single declarative sentence, the argument is not ready.

The argument is two to four paragraphs that develop the claim. Each paragraph should do one thing. “One paragraph, one move” is the rule you will hear from every op-ed coach, and it is the rule most first drafts violate.

The evidence is where your data lives. One chart, one number, one case, a named source. You are not proving the argument the way a paper would; you are giving the reader enough to trust you. A single well-chosen statistic with a live hyperlink is worth a paragraph of hedged prose.

The call to action is the last paragraph. What should happen, and who should make it happen? “Policymakers should consider” is the call to action of a piece that has no call to action. “The Boulder City Council votes Tuesday on an ordinance that would require X; it should vote yes” is a call to action. Specificity is the whole game.

Hook, nut graf, argument, evidence, call. Print it on an index card. Pin it above your monitor.

19.5 Pitching is half the work

Editors do not read op-eds they have not been pitched. This is the single most common failure mode among first-time authors: they write a brilliant 800 words, attach it to an email that says “Please consider the attached for your opinion page,” and wonder why they never hear back. The attached draft is the easy half. The three sentences in the email body are the hard half.

A working pitch email has a specific shape. The subject line names the genre and the angle: “Op-ed pitch: what Boulder’s eviction data actually shows.” The body opens with the argument in one sentence. The second sentence says why the piece is timely (there is a vote, a ruling, an anniversary, a report). The third sentence says why you are the person to write it: your credential is not your degree, it is the work you have already done on the question. Paste the full draft below a horizontal rule in the email body. Attach it as well, ideally as .docx and .txt. Put your phone number in the signature. If the editor wants the piece, they want to call you today.

TipThe Missing Manual

Op-ed editors want a pitch email and a draft that is one click from publishable. Submitting a Google Doc with Track Changes, or a PDF, or a link to a Notion page, is a rookie mistake that tells the editor you do not know the genre. Paste the full text into the email body. Attach the draft as .docx for the copy desk. Include a one-sentence bio and a phone number. Do not send a 500-word cover letter. Do not apologize for the length of your pitch. Do not ask whether they would be interested before sending the draft: by the time they decide, they have already moved on. The other piece of infrastructure most authors skip: a single embedded chart. Outlets that can publish a chart (and most can, even the online editions of print dailies) report that op-eds with one chart get substantially more social-share traffic than op-eds without one. Make the chart non-optional.

19.6 A worked example: the Boulder audit as an op-ed

Take the dataset you have been building since the earlier chapters. It does not matter which one: the HMIS audit from Chapter 9, the campaign-finance scrape from Chapter 11, the Alpine-Balsam housing analysis from Chapter 14, or your own. You have a finding. You have evidence. You have, almost certainly, a moment when a public body is about to decide something your finding is relevant to. An op-ed is the translation.

Suppose your audit found that the Boulder County homelessness response system (as documented in HMIS) routes single adults to shelter beds in a way that systematically disadvantages households with recent non-Colorado addresses. You have the numbers. You have the code. You have Chapter 9’s disaggregated accuracy report. What you need is a 800-word piece for the Boulder Daily Camera or the Colorado Sun, pegged to the next Boulder City Council agenda item on homelessness services funding, that gives a reader who will never open your notebook a reason to call their council member.

Your hook is the upcoming vote, or the specific person turned away. Your nut graf says, in one sentence, that the scoring system is producing a pattern its designers did not intend and the Council should pause the contract renewal to investigate. Your argument is two paragraphs on how the scoring works and one paragraph on what the data shows. Your evidence is the chart below. Your call is: postpone the December vote, commission an external audit, and release the vendor’s scoring logic under CORA (the Colorado Open Records Act; see Chapter 12).

Here is the chart. One chart. You will not get two.

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Rates are illustrative; replace with your audit's actual figures.
df = pd.DataFrame({
    "group": ["In-state, 1+ yr", "In-state, <1 yr", "Out-of-state"],
    "referral_rate": [0.58, 0.41, 0.23],
    "n": [812, 294, 187],
})

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 3.8))
bars = ax.bar(df["group"], df["referral_rate"],
              color=["#2b6cb0", "#2b6cb0", "#c53030"])
for bar, n in zip(bars, df["n"]):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
            bar.get_height() + 0.015,
            f"n={n}", ha="center", fontsize=9)
ax.set_ylabel("Share referred to shelter within 7 days")
ax.set_ylim(0, 0.75)
ax.set_title("Boulder County HMIS referral rates by prior-address tenure,\n"
             "January 2023 through June 2025")
ax.spines["top"].set_visible(False)
ax.spines["right"].set_visible(False)
fig.tight_layout()
fig.savefig("ch17_hmis_referral.png", dpi=160, bbox_inches="tight")

Two things about that chart are deliberate. The title is a complete sentence, because a newsroom reader looks at the title first and the axes second. The colors code the argument (one group is the outlier), because a newsroom reader will not parse a legend. The sample sizes are on the bars, because a newsroom reader who does not see them will assume the disparity is noise. The chart earns its place in 800 words because it does in one image what a paragraph of prose would fail to do.

A note on rendering. Editors accept .docx, .txt, and pasted-into-email plain text. They do not accept .qmd, .ipynb, or .tex. Render your draft to Word with quarto render draft.qmd --to docx, and keep a plain-text version in the email body so the editor can read it without an attachment. For citations, the web convention is in-text hyperlinks rather than footnotes: link the phrase “the county’s own data” to the dataset or the audit. Do not include a bibliography. If the editor wants a source list, they will ask.

19.7 Why this matters for the book’s argument

An op-ed is, among other things, an exercise in openness as argument rather than openness as data release. Chapter 8 taught you to publish a dataset with a data dictionary and a durable identifier. That is necessary and insufficient. A dataset nobody reads, like a report nobody reads, is openness in form without openness in effect. An op-ed is how you make the dataset’s implication publicly legible; it is the reason the data dictionary existed in the first place.

NoteIn the Public Interest

Transparency is not self-executing. Chapter 8 built an installed base of documented, citable data; this chapter is where that base meets a reader. An op-ed that lands in the Daily Camera the week before a council vote does more work for the public interest than the same finding published as a 40-page report the council staff will skim in a briefing (compare Chapter 20). You will write both. The difference between a data release that changes a vote and one that does not is almost always a 750-word argument somebody pitched to an editor who picked up the phone.

19.8 Exercises

Exercise 17.1 (Analytic). Pick three recent op-eds on AI or data policy, at least one from a data scientist writing for a general audience. Start with Emily Bender (Bender 2023) on large language models, Deborah Raji (Raji 2020) on how data encodes racism, and Meredith Broussard (Broussard 2023) on algorithmic bias; substitute equivalents if those links have rotted. For each, identify the hook (first sentence), the nut graf (one sentence that states the argument), and the call to action (what the author wants the reader to do, and who the reader is). Produce a two-page annotated comparison. Note: at least one of your three op-eds will not have a crisp nut graf. Say so.

Exercise 17.2 (Core artifact, part 1). Draft a 750 to 1,000 word op-ed based on the audit, dataset, or analysis you built earlier in the book. Target a specific, named outlet: the Boulder Daily Camera, the Colorado Sun, The Conversation US, Inside Higher Ed, or an instructor-approved equivalent. Your draft must include a hook, a nut graf, a specific call to action, and at least one in-text hyperlink to source data. Save it as exercises/ch17_oped_draft.md and render to exercises/ch17_oped_draft.docx. Word count is enforced. A 1,200-word op-ed will not be published; a 600-word op-ed is not yet finished.

Exercise 17.3 (Core artifact, part 2). Write the pitch email that accompanies your draft. Three sentences in the body: argument, timeliness, credential. Paste the full draft below a horizontal rule. Include an appropriate subject line, a one-sentence bio, and a phone number. Save it as exercises/ch17_pitch_email.md. The email must be one a real editor at your target outlet could read in under ninety seconds and decide to act on.

Exercise 17.4 (Core artifact, part 3). Produce one embedded chart for the op-ed. Build it in matplotlib, seaborn, or plotnine; export at a size that will survive being shrunk to 500 pixels wide. The chart’s title must be a full declarative sentence. The chart must carry its sample sizes. The chart must work in grayscale (test by saving as black-and-white and confirming the argument survives). Save as exercises/ch17_oped_chart.png at 160 dpi.

Exercise 17.5 (Open, moment-of-publication). Op-eds live or die by timing. Watch the local and national news cycle for a week. Identify the moment, real or plausible, when your op-ed would be most likely to land: a council vote, a court ruling, an anniversary, a federal action. Rewrite the lede (the hook plus the first paragraph) for that moment. Save both ledes (the original and the retimed version) and write 200 words on what changed and why. If the moment arrives within the window of the course, send the pitch.

Exercises 17.2, 17.3, and 17.4 together constitute the Part V artifact for this chapter. At the end of the week you will have a file in the course’s submission repository that is, without further work, a complete op-ed package you could send tomorrow. Some of you will send it. Some of yours will be published. The draft that is not published goes into the portfolio alongside the five artifacts from the remaining Part V chapters.

19.9 Looking ahead

The op-ed reaches audiences the report never will. The report, for a different audience, makes a case the op-ed cannot. Chapter 20 takes up the longer form: how to structure a 20-to-40 page argument for a policy reader, how to keep the figures reproducible under a build system, and how to write the two-page executive summary that is, in practice, the only part most readers will read. The skills carry forward. The pitch-mindset (know your reader, make every element earn its place) carries forward most of all. Chapter 22 and Chapter 24 extend it into genres where the audience is a committee chair and a permanent record, respectively, and the stakes of getting the framing right are proportionally higher.

## The anatomy of an 800-word argument

An op-ed is not a short paper. It is an argument with a particular load-bearing structure, and editors can diagnose whether the structure is there within sixty seconds of opening your attachment. Five parts:

**The hook** is the first sentence, or at most the first two. It is the news peg, the anecdote, the surprising number. Its job is to earn the next paragraph.

**The nut graf** (from journalism; the paragraph that says "in a nutshell") is where you tell the reader what your argument is. Not what the piece is about. What you want the reader to believe by the end. If you cannot write your nut graf as a single declarative sentence, the argument is not ready.

**The argument** is two to four paragraphs that develop the claim. Each paragraph should do one thing. "One paragraph, one move" is the rule you will hear from every op-ed coach, and it is the rule most first drafts violate.

**The evidence** is where your data lives. One chart, one number, one case, a named source. You are not proving the argument the way a paper would; you are giving the reader enough to trust you. A single well-chosen statistic with a live hyperlink is worth a paragraph of hedged prose.

**The call to action** is the last paragraph. What should happen, and who should make it happen? "Policymakers should consider" is the call to action of a piece that has no call to action. "The Boulder City Council votes Tuesday on an ordinance that would require X; it should vote yes" is a call to action. Specificity is the whole game.

Hook, nut graf, argument, evidence, call. Print it on an index card. Pin it above your monitor.

This is the single most common failure mode among first-time authors: they write a brilliant 800 words, attach it to an email that says "Please consider the attached for your opinion page," and wonder why they never hear back. A working pitch email has a specific shape. The subject line names the genre and the angle: "Op-ed pitch: what Boulder's eviction data actually shows." The body opens with the argument in one sentence. The second sentence says why the piece is timely (there is a vote, a ruling, an anniversary, a report). The third sentence says why you are the person to write it: your credential is not your degree, it is the work you have already done on the question. Paste the full draft below a horizontal rule in the email body. Attach it as well, ideally as `.docx` and `.txt`. Put your phone number in the signature. If the editor wants the piece, they want to call you today.

::: {.callout-tip title="The Missing Manual"}
Op-ed editors want a pitch email and a draft that is one click from publishable. Submitting a Google Doc with Track Changes, or a PDF, or a link to a Notion page, is a rookie mistake that tells the editor you do not know the genre. Paste the full text into the email body. Attach the draft as `.docx` for the copy desk. Include a one-sentence bio and a phone number. Do not send a 500-word cover letter. Do not apologize for the length of your pitch. Do not ask whether they would be interested before sending the draft: by the time they decide, they have already moved on. The other piece of infrastructure most authors skip: a single embedded chart. Outlets that can publish a chart (and most can, even the online editions of print dailies) report that op-eds with one chart get substantially more social-share traffic than op-eds without one. Make the chart non-optional.
:::

## A worked example: the Boulder audit as an op-ed

Take the dataset you have been building since the earlier chapters. It does not matter which one: the HMIS audit from @sec-ch-07-oversight, the campaign-finance scrape from @sec-ch-09-journalism, the Alpine-Balsam housing analysis from @sec-ch-12-planning, or your own. You have a finding. You have evidence. You have, almost certainly, a moment when a public body is about to decide something your finding is relevant to. An op-ed is the translation.

Suppose your audit found that the Boulder County homelessness response system (as documented in HMIS) routes single adults to shelter beds in a way that systematically disadvantages households with recent non-Colorado addresses. You have the numbers. You have the code. You have @sec-ch-07-oversight's disaggregated accuracy report. What you need is a 800-word piece for the *Boulder Daily Camera* or the *Colorado Sun*, pegged to the next Boulder City Council agenda item on homelessness services funding, that gives a reader who will never open your notebook a reason to call their council member.

Your hook is the upcoming vote, or the specific person turned away. Your nut graf says, in one sentence, that the scoring system is producing a pattern its designers did not intend and the Council should pause the contract renewal to investigate. Your argument is two paragraphs on how the scoring works and one paragraph on what the data shows. Your evidence is the chart below. Your call is: postpone the December vote, commission an external audit, and release the vendor's scoring logic under CORA (the Colorado Open Records Act; see @sec-ch-10-law).

Here is the chart. One chart. You will not get two.

```python
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Rates are illustrative; replace with your audit's actual figures.
df = pd.DataFrame({
    "group": ["In-state, 1+ yr", "In-state, <1 yr", "Out-of-state"],
    "referral_rate": [0.58, 0.41, 0.23],
    "n": [812, 294, 187],
})

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 3.8))
bars = ax.bar(df["group"], df["referral_rate"],
              color=["#2b6cb0", "#2b6cb0", "#c53030"])
for bar, n in zip(bars, df["n"]):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
            bar.get_height() + 0.015,
            f"n={n}", ha="center", fontsize=9)
ax.set_ylabel("Share referred to shelter within 7 days")
ax.set_ylim(0, 0.75)
ax.set_title("Boulder County HMIS referral rates by prior-address tenure,\n"
             "January 2023 through June 2025")
ax.spines["top"].set_visible(False)
ax.spines["right"].set_visible(False)
fig.tight_layout()
fig.savefig("ch17_hmis_referral.png", dpi=160, bbox_inches="tight")
```

Two things about that chart are deliberate. The title is a complete sentence, because a newsroom reader looks at the title first and the axes second. The colors code the argument (one group is the outlier), because a newsroom reader will not parse a legend. The sample sizes are on the bars, because a newsroom reader who does not see them will assume the disparity is noise. The chart earns its place in 800 words because it does in one image what a paragraph of prose would fail to do.

A note on rendering. Editors accept `.docx`, `.txt`, and pasted-into-email plain text. They do not accept `.qmd`, `.ipynb`, or `.tex`. Render your draft to Word with `quarto render draft.qmd --to docx`, and keep a plain-text version in the email body so the editor can read it without an attachment. For citations, the web convention is in-text hyperlinks rather than footnotes: link the phrase "the county's own data" to the dataset or the audit. Do not include a bibliography. If the editor wants a source list, they will ask.

## Why this matters for the book's argument

An op-ed is, among other things, an exercise in openness as argument rather than openness as data release. @sec-ch-06-openness taught you to publish a dataset with a data dictionary and a durable identifier. That is necessary and insufficient. A dataset nobody reads, like a report nobody reads, is openness in form without openness in effect. An op-ed is how you make the dataset's implication publicly legible; it is the reason the data dictionary existed in the first place.

::: {.callout-note title="In the Public Interest"}
Transparency is not self-executing. @sec-ch-06-openness built an installed base of documented, citable data; this chapter is where that base meets a reader. An op-ed that lands in the *Daily Camera* the week before a council vote does more work for the public interest than the same finding published as a 40-page report the council staff will skim in a briefing (compare @sec-ch-18-reports). You will write both. The difference between a data release that changes a vote and one that does not is almost always a 750-word argument somebody pitched to an editor who picked up the phone.
:::

## Looking ahead

The op-ed reaches audiences the report never will. The report, for a different audience, makes a case the op-ed cannot. @sec-ch-18-reports takes up the longer form: how to structure a 20-to-40 page argument for a policy reader, how to keep the figures reproducible under a build system, and how to write the two-page executive summary that is, in practice, the only part most readers will read. The skills carry forward. The pitch-mindset (know your reader, make every element earn its place) carries forward most of all. @sec-ch-20-testimony and @sec-ch-22-archival-deposits extend it into genres where the audience is a committee chair and a permanent record, respectively, and the stakes of getting the framing right are proportionally higher.

- The Op-Ed Project, <https://www.theopedproject.org/>. Handbook, byline survey, and pitch-coaching network. If you read only one external resource from this chapter, read this one.
- *The Conversation US*, pitch guidelines: <https://theconversation.com/us/pitches>. The most accessible outlet in the United States for academic data scientists. Pieces are published under Creative Commons and routinely picked up by regional papers.
- Emily M. Bender, representative guest essay on large language models: [@bender2023]. A model of how to write a data-science op-ed for a general audience without flattening the technical argument.
- Inioluwa Deborah Raji, "How our data encodes systematic racism," *MIT Technology Review*, December 2020: [@raji2022]. A model of a data-scientist op-ed that leads with a case and a number, not a methodology.
- Meredith Broussard, guest essay on algorithmic bias: [@broussard2023]. See also her book [@broussard2018] for the longer argument.
- *The Associated Press Stylebook* [@apstylebook]. The house style used by most U.S. newsrooms. Read the entries on numbers, dates, and attribution before your first pitch.
- Poynter, op-ed how-to resources: [@poynter_oped]. Craft-level advice from the journalism industry's in-house trainer.
- Kathleen Kingsbury, "Why we're retiring the term op-ed," *The New York Times*, April 2021: [@kingsbury2021]. Context on the genre's current self-understanding at the paper that invented it.
- ProPublica's "Machine Bias" series [@angwin2016]. Not op-eds, but the kind of reporting that the best data-scientist op-eds cite and translate for a general reader.
- Keegan [-@keegan2026], "Public interest data infrastructuring." The framework this book extends. An op-ed is the smallest unit of public infrastructure you will build in this course; read the paper for the larger unit.
